Frequently Asked Questions

Everything you need to know about yacht and marine insurance in New Zealand

Navigating yacht and boat insurance in New Zealand can feel overwhelming, especially if you're a first-time boat owner or switching providers. Below we've compiled the most common questions our visitors ask about marine insurance coverage, costs, claims, and policy features. If you can't find the answer you're looking for, feel free to contact us directly for personalised advice from a qualified NZ marine insurance adviser.

1What does yacht insurance cover in NZ?
Yacht insurance in New Zealand typically covers hull damage, liability protection, theft, and loss of the vessel. Most policies include coverage for collision, sinking, capsizing, and storm damage. Additional coverage options may include personal effects, medical expenses for crew, towing and salvage assistance, and professional fees. It's important to review your specific policy as coverage varies between providers and policy types.
2Do I need insurance for my yacht in New Zealand?
While yacht insurance is not legally mandated in New Zealand, it is strongly recommended. If you have a mortgage on your vessel, your lender will require comprehensive insurance. Marine third-party liability insurance is essential for protecting yourself against claims from other parties. Most marinas and mooring facilities also require proof of insurance before allowing your vessel to be based there.
3What types of vessels can be insured?
NZ marine insurers cover a wide range of vessels including sailboats, motorboats, catamarans, multihulls, cabin cruisers, day boats, and fishing vessels. Specialist policies are available for jet skis, personal watercraft, and small recreational boats. Commercial fishing vessels and charter boats have dedicated insurance products. Some insurers also provide coverage for historic or classic vessels with agreed valuation options.
4How much does yacht insurance cost in NZ?
Yacht insurance costs in New Zealand vary significantly based on vessel value, age, type, and intended use. Premiums typically range from 0.5% to 2% of the insured value annually. Factors affecting cost include your claim history, crew experience, security features, and where the vessel is moored. It's worth comparing quotes from multiple insurers as premium variations can be substantial for the same coverage level.
5What is marine third-party liability?
Marine third-party liability insurance protects you financially if you're found legally responsible for damage, injury, or death to other people or their property while operating your vessel. This includes damage to other boats, injuries to swimmers or other water users, and incidents at the dock. In New Zealand waters, liability claims can be substantial, making this coverage critical for all boat owners.
6Does yacht insurance cover racing?
Standard yacht insurance policies typically exclude racing or competitive events. However, most NZ insurers offer specialist racing yacht insurance that provides comprehensive coverage during competition. Racing coverage usually includes hull protection during races, liability during events, and coverage for racing-specific incidents. If you plan to race your vessel, notify your insurer and ensure you have appropriate racing coverage in place.
7Am I covered if I sail offshore?
Coverage for offshore cruising depends on your specific policy and the distance from New Zealand waters. Most standard policies cover cruising within a defined geographic zone, typically including the Tasman Sea and Australian waters. Extended offshore coverage is available for blue water cruising to the Pacific Islands and beyond. You must declare your intended cruising area to your insurer and may need to upgrade your policy for extended passages.
8What happens if my yacht is damaged in a storm?
If your yacht is damaged in a storm and you have comprehensive hull coverage, you can lodge a claim with your insurer. Document all damage with photographs and obtain repair quotes. Your insurer will assess the claim and typically arrange repairs through approved repairers or reimburse you for authorised repairs. Storm damage claims are common in NZ and most policies have provisions for emergency repairs to prevent further deterioration.
9Can I insure a jet ski or personal watercraft?
Yes, jet skis and personal watercraft can be insured with specialist policies available through most NZ marine insurers. These policies typically cover theft, damage, liability, and loss of the vessel. Coverage may be limited to specific age ranges or brand models. Jet ski insurance often includes mandatory safety equipment requirements and may be more affordable as an add-on to an existing boat insurance policy.
10How do I make a claim on my yacht insurance?
To make a claim, contact your insurer as soon as possible after an incident occurs. Most NZ insurers have 24/7 claims hotlines. Provide detailed information about what happened, including photographs of any damage. Your insurer will assign a claims assessor who will investigate and determine coverage. Keep all receipts and documentation for repairs. Claims are typically assessed within 10-15 business days.

Understanding Yacht Insurance in New Zealand

New Zealand is a maritime nation with over 1.6 million recreational vessels registered across the country. From the Bay of Islands to the Marlborough Sounds, Kiwi boat owners enjoy some of the best cruising grounds in the world. However, with this privilege comes the responsibility of properly insuring your vessel against the unique risks of New Zealand waters — from unpredictable weather patterns to the specific navigational hazards found around our coastline.

Marine insurance in New Zealand is regulated by the Insurance Council of New Zealand (ICNZ) and governed by the Marine Insurance Act 1908, which sets out the legal framework for marine insurance contracts. Unlike car insurance, marine insurance is not compulsory in New Zealand. However, most lenders, marinas, and mooring facilities require proof of insurance before they will allow your vessel to be stored or financed through them. Understanding what your policy covers — and what it excludes — is essential for making an informed decision.
